Subtheme:
Fatal Accidental Overdose In Allegheny County From 2007 to 2025

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. GENERAL TREND
    How has the number of fatal overdoses changed over time?

    Motivations:
    I wanted to know if COVID has any impact on fatal overdose increase.
    I also wondered if season change has any impact on fatal overdose increase as well.

  2. GEOGRAPHIC
    Are there geographic hotspots of overdoses?
    Following up: How does it change over time?
    Motivations:
    Because I personally live in Allegheny County, I want to know if there’s any hotspot near me.
    (and if CMU is an overdose hotspot, I guess it is).

  3. GEOGRAPHIC
    Which drugs are most commonly associated with fatal overdoses?
    Following Up: How has the presence of the frequently involved drugs changed over time?
    And how often do overdoses involve multiple substances? Single substance?
    Motivations:
    When I was in high school in Maryland, several fatal overdoses happened in my community. As an international student with little prior knowledge of drugs in the U.S., those incidents left a strong impression on me and made me want to better understand which substances can be deadly.

Discoveries & Insights

We begin the exploration by understanding the timely trend of fatal accidental overdose, studying individual variables, and addressing data quality issues.

GEOGRAPHIC DRUGS

We began with inspecting the chemical name of drugs using filter in excel, and we discovered the following data quality issue:
- Inconsistent naming: 1,1-Difluoroethane and Difluoroethane as two separate entries are likely the same chemical but with different naming.
- Multiple substances in one field: “Citalopram/Escitalopram” two drugs reported in one entry instead of separate fields
- Both drug classes and specific substances appeared as entries: Synthetic Cannabinoid vs. 4-fluoro-MDMB-BINACA (a specific synthetic cannabinoid)
- Non-drug or unclear entries like Carbon Monoxide, which is technically a cause of death, but not a “drug” in the conventional sense.
After identifying the data issues, we began exploring what are the most frequently involved drugs in fatal accidental overdose in Allegheny County from 2007 to 2025.

Summary

In this project I looked at when, where, and what’s involved in fatal overdoses in Allegheny County (2007–2025). Yearly totals rise to a clear peak in 2016–2017, stay high for a while, and start to dip after 2023. Counts are lower on Monday–Tuesday and higher toward the weekend, especially Saturday. By time of day, the low point is around 2–5 AM; numbers climb after 6 AM and are highest from late morning into the afternoon. I don’t see a strong month-to-month seasonal pattern.

On the map, 15210 and 15212 stand out as persistent hotspots, with several nearby ZIPs as secondary clusters. Fentanyl leads the substance list, and most cases involve more than one drug. Men appear more often than women, and most cases are in people in their 30s–50s. I standardized some drug names; and 2025 is still updating. If I continue, I’d normalize by population, dig into common drug combinations.
